Discovery -> Diagnosis -> Decision

A complete framework for Rainmaker and Mastermind sales calls.

# HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT
This is a working playbook, not a word-for-word script. The language in quotes is suggested phrasing you should internalize and make your own. The frameworks are non-negotiable - follow the structure, hit every required beat, and use the decision gates to stay on track.

The document is organized into six sections:

The Call Framework  - The 7 phases of a sales call with required beats and time targets
Objection Handling Playbook  - Exact frameworks for the top objections

CORE PRINCIPLE: Your job is not to convince people to buy. Your job is to help qualified prospects make a decision they are already leaning toward. The framework diagnoses the problem, quantifies the cost of inaction, and presents the solution. If the prospect is qualified and the pain is real, the close is the natural conclusion - not a pressure tactic.

# SECTION 1: PRE-CALL QUALIFICATION FRAMEWORK

Purpose: Filter out information-gathering prospects before they consume 45-70 minutes of closer time. The setter's job is not just to book a call - it's to book the RIGHT call with a prospect who has a real problem, real urgency, and real capacity to invest.
## The Problem We Are Solving
In our analysis of recent sales calls, 3 out of 4 lost deals were information-gathering prospects who had no intention of making a decision on the call. They consumed an average of 62 minutes each. The cost: roughly 3 hours of closer time per week on calls that will never close. Better setter qualification is the single highest-ROI fix available.
## Setter Qualification Questions
After confirming basic fit (they are a PT, ATC, or rehab clinician interested in cash-based practice), the setter asks these five questions:

Question 1: Stage Assessment
Setter: "Where are you at right now with your practice? Are you still in the idea phase, have you already started seeing patients, or do you have an established business you're looking to grow?"

Question 2: Urgency Gauge
Setter: "What's happening right now that made you reach out to us? Was there something specific that triggered this, or are you just exploring options?"

[GREEN] GREEN LIGHT: Specific trigger: "I'm turning away patients," "I just quit my job," "I need to hire and I've never done it," "I'm stuck at $X and can't break through."
[RED FLAG] RED FLAG: "Just exploring," "Just wanted to learn more," "I saw your ad and thought I'd check it out." This signals information-gathering. Does not disqualify, but lowers priority and the closer needs to know.

Question 3: Decision-Maker Check
Setter: "If this turns out to be a great fit for you, are you in a position to make a decision on your own, or is there someone else who would need to be part of that conversation?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: If there is a spouse or partner involved in financial decisions, note this for the closer. The closer needs to either get both decision-makers on the call or prepare the spouse objection handling in advance.

Question 4: Timing
Setter: "If we could show you a clear path to [their stated goal], how soon would you want to get started? Are we talking weeks, months, or is this more of a someday thing?"

[GREEN] GREEN LIGHT: "As soon as possible," "This month," "I'm ready."
[RED FLAG] RED FLAG: "Maybe next year," "Just gathering info for now," "Someday." Flag for closer - this prospect needs urgency creation or may not be call-ready.

Question 5: Investment Awareness
Setter: "I want to make sure I'm respectful of your time. This call is designed to figure out if we're the right fit to help you, and if so, to walk you through what it looks like to work with us, including the investment. It's not a free consulting call. Are you comfortable with that?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: This is the single most important qualification question. It sets the expectation that the call is a decision-making call, not an information-gathering session. If the prospect says "Well, I just wanted to pick your brain" or "I'm not looking to invest right now," the setter can redirect: "No problem at all. We do have a ton of free content including our podcast and YouTube channel. Let me send you some links. When you're at a point where you're ready to invest in getting help, we'd love to have this conversation."

# SECTION 2: THE CALL FRAMEWORK

The call has seven phases. Each phase has required beats, a time target, and a decision gate. The phases must happen in order. Do not skip phases. Do not jump to the platform demo before completing discovery and gap creation.

Total target call time: 40-55 minutes. If a call exceeds 60 minutes, something went wrong - usually the platform demo ran too long or objections were not addressed efficiently.

## Phase 1: Connection & Agenda (3-5 minutes)
Goal: Build rapport, acknowledge the setter's notes, and set the agenda for the call so the prospect knows what to expect.

Required Beats:
Personal connection. Use something from the setter notes or their location/background. 2-3 sentences max. Don't force it.
Acknowledge what you know. "Jack shared some notes with me - it sounds like you're [brief summary of their situation]. Is that right?"
Set the agenda. This is the most important beat in Phase 1.

Closer: "Here's how I'd like to use our time today. First, I want to learn more about you, your business, and what you're trying to accomplish. Then, if it seems like we're a good fit to help, I'll walk you through how we work with people in your situation and what the investment looks like. And then we'll figure out together whether this makes sense for you. Sound good?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: This agenda-setting line does three critical things: (1) It tells the prospect this is a two-way evaluation, not a pitch. (2) It signals that pricing will be discussed, so they are not blindsided later. (3) It frames the call as ending with a decision, not "more information." If a prospect pushes back on this structure, that is diagnostic information - they may be an information-gatherer.

## Phase 2: Discovery (10-15 minutes)
Goal: Understand their situation at three levels: the facts (numbers, stage, logistics), the feelings (frustration, fear, excitement), and the future (vision, goals, what success looks like). You need all three to create an effective gap and present a compelling solution.

THE DISCOVERY RULE: Never prescribe before you diagnose. You are a clinician - you would never walk into a patient room and start treating before completing an evaluation. The same applies here. Resist the urge to jump into solutions, platform demos, or "we can help with that" statements during discovery. Your only job in this phase is to ask questions and listen.

Level 1: The Facts
Adapt based on what you already know from the setter notes. Don't re-ask questions the setter already covered.

For Rainmaker prospects (pre-launch or early stage):
Where are you working now? Full-time? What are you making?
Have you taken any steps yet toward starting your practice? (LLC, space, seeing patients on the side?)
Do you have a space in mind, or is that still open?
What does your support system look like? Partner, family - are they on board?
What kind of patients do you want to work with?

For Mastermind prospects (established, scaling):
What was your total revenue last year? What are you averaging per month right now?
How many new evals per month are you getting?
Are you selling plan-of-care packages? What does your average plan of care look like?
Do you have any recurring revenue or continuity memberships?
What is your team right now? Just you, or do you have staff?
What are your overhead costs? Rent, payroll, marketing spend?
Where are your patients coming from? What marketing are you doing?

Level 2: The Feelings
This is where most closers fall short. The facts tell you what the business looks like. The feelings tell you why they got on this call. You need both.

Closer: "So you've been at this for [X time] and you're at [revenue/situation]. How does that feel? Is that where you thought you'd be at this point?"

Closer: "What's the hardest part about where you're at right now? Not the business problem - I mean for you personally. What keeps you up at night?"

Closer: "If nothing changes in the next 12 months, and you're in the same spot a year from now, what does that mean for you? For your family?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: Listen for emotional fuel: resentment ("I'm building revenue for someone else"), fear ("I don't know how long I can keep this up"), frustration ("Everyone tells me I'm close but I don't feel close"), desperation ("I'm barely scraping by"). When you hear these, reflect them back. Do NOT gloss over them with "sure" or "yeah" and move on. These are the emotional anchors that will drive the close.

Level 3: The Future
Closer: "Let's fast-forward. If everything goes right over the next 12 months, what does your life look like? What does your practice look like? What does a typical week look like for you?"

Closer: "Why is that important to you? Not the business goal - the life goal. What does hitting that number actually change for you and your family?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: The prospect's future vision is the emotional anchor for the close. When they say "I want to work 20 hours a week and make double what I'm making now" - you'll reference this later: "You told me you want to work 20 hours a week and double your income. Everything I'm about to show you is designed to get you exactly there."

## Phase 3: Gap Creation (5-10 minutes)
Goal: Make the gap between where they are and where they want to be vivid, measurable, and emotionally uncomfortable. This is the phase that creates urgency. Without it, comfortable prospects stay comfortable and say "let me think about it."

The Math Exercise (Adapt to Prospect Stage)

For Rainmaker prospects:
Closer: "So right now you're making [$X] at your job. Let's look at what this could look like in your own practice. In the cash-based space, a healthy rate is about $200 an hour. If you work just 20 hours a week seeing patients, that's $16,000 a month. That's [X times] what you're taking home now, in fewer hours. What would that mean for you?"

For Mastermind prospects:
Closer: "You're at [$X/month] right now. Your goal is [$Y/month]. That's a gap of [$Z/month], or [$Z x 12] per year that's on the table. Let me show you where that gap is coming from..."

Then identify the specific revenue levers that are underperforming: plan-of-care conversion rate, average revenue per visit, new evals per month, continuity revenue percentage, capacity utilization. Use their own numbers against the benchmarks.

The Cost of Inaction
After the math exercise, make the cost of doing nothing concrete:

Closer: "So there's [$Z per year] sitting on the table right now. Every month that goes by without fixing [the specific gap], that's another [$Z/12] you're leaving behind. If you spend the next 6 months trying to figure this out on your own, that's [$Z/2] in lost revenue. Does that feel acceptable to you?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: Do NOT accept surface-level answers to the cost-of-inaction question. If they say "Well, I'd still be okay" or "There's some things to think about," press deeper: "You told me earlier that [emotional anchor - their own words about frustration, fear, or desire]. If nothing changes, you're still in that same place a year from now. Is that really okay with you?" The prospect's own words are the most powerful gap-creation tool you have.

The Skills Assessment (Mastermind Only)
For Mastermind prospects, run a quick constraint assessment. Rate each area 1-10 (1 = critical need, 10 = no help needed):
Getting enough new patients consistently
Converting evaluations into plan-of-care commitments
Building recurring/continuity revenue
Hiring and managing staff
Working ON the business (not just IN it)
Financial clarity and confidence

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: Any score of 3 or below is a weapon. Call it out: "You gave hiring a 1 out of 10. That means this is the single biggest thing standing between you and your goal. You've been in business 5 years and haven't solved it yet. What's stopped you?" Do not treat this assessment as a checklist. Treat it as a diagnostic tool that reveals where the pain is.

## Phase 4: Temperature Check (2-3 minutes)
Goal: Gauge whether the prospect is ready to hear the solution. This is a mandatory gate. Do not skip it. Do not proceed to the platform demo if the temperature is below 7.

Closer: "Based on everything we've talked about so far - where you are, where you want to be, and what's in the way - on a scale of 1 to 10, how important is it to you to solve this problem in the next few months?"

HARD RULE: If the temperature check is 5 or below, do NOT proceed to the platform demo. You will waste 15-20 minutes presenting to someone who is not going to buy today. Either go back to discovery to find the pain you missed, or wrap up the call gracefully. Every minute spent demoing to a cold prospect is a minute stolen from a warm one.

## Phase 5: Solution Presentation (10-15 minutes)
Goal: Present the program as the bridge that closes the gap you just identified. Every feature you mention should connect back to a specific problem or goal the prospect shared in discovery. If a feature does not connect to their situation, skip it.

Transition from Gap to Solution:
Closer: "So here's where you are: [summary of current situation]. Here's where you want to be: [their stated goal]. And the gap is [specific problems identified]. Let me show you exactly how we help people in your situation close that gap."

The Platform Walkthrough:
Calibrate the walkthrough length to prospect temperature:
Hot prospect (8-10): 5-7 minute overview. Hit the highlights, connect each to their situation, move to price. They're buying - don't oversell.
Warm prospect (6-7): 10-12 minute walkthrough. Spend more time on the features that address their specific concerns. Use stories.
Cool prospect (if they somehow got past the gate): Focus on 2-3 features that directly solve their stated problem. Do not tour the entire platform.

Connecting Features to Their Situation:
For every feature you show, use this formula:

"You told me [their problem]. This is how we solve that. [Feature]. And here's what that looks like in practice for someone in your situation..."

Your Personal Story (Required)
During the solution presentation, deploy at least one personal transformation story. This is the single most effective selling tool across all calls analyzed.

If you are a clinician who has been through the program:
Share your own journey. Where you started, what changed, where you are now. Be specific with numbers and timelines. Example: "When I joined the Mastermind, I was making about $4,000 a month. My first month, I made $10,000. By month three, I was at $15,000. And I'm in a small town."

If you are not a clinician:
Use client transformation stories. You need 3-5 stories memorized and ready to deploy, matched to common prospect profiles. See Section 6 for the story bank template. The key is specificity: names (with permission), starting revenue, current revenue, timeline, and the specific thing that changed.

Program Routing:

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: If a prospect is on the border (full-time, $3-5K/month, has proof of concept), present both options and let them choose. Explain the difference clearly. If in doubt, Mastermind is the better recommendation for someone who is full-time and committed, because the accountability and community are stronger. Do not default to Rainmaker just because it is the cheaper option.

## Phase 6: Investment & Close (5-10 minutes)
Goal: Present the investment, handle objections, and ask for a decision. This is not the time for new information. Everything the prospect needs to make a decision should already be on the table from Phases 2-5.

Price Presentation - Rainmaker:
Closer: "The investment for the Clinical Rainmaker program is $7,800 for the full 6 months. Everything we just talked about is included - your one-on-one coach, the group coaching calls, the full curriculum, the community access, all of it. No upsells, no hidden fees."

Closer: "Most people break it into payments: $1,800 to get started, and then $1,200 per month for the remaining 5 months. It's interest-free and internal - no financing, no credit check. And if you'd rather pay in full, you can absolutely do that too."

Price Presentation - Mastermind:
Closer: "The investment for the Mastermind is $1,599 per month for 12 months. There's a one-time $2,200 onboarding fee in the first month, so your first month is $3,800, and then it's $1,599 each month after that."

STRUCTURED INCENTIVE (Workshop/Webinar Attendees Only): "Because you attended [the workshop/webinar], we're able to waive the $2,200 onboarding fee. So instead of $3,800 to start, it's $1,599 to get going, and then $1,599 each month from there." Present this as an attendee benefit, not a closer concession. It is available ONLY to workshop/webinar attendees. Do not offer it to cold prospects.

The Reframe:
Closer: "To put that in perspective, $1,599 a month is about one patient buying a plan of care. That's it. One patient per month covers your entire investment. And our average member adds significantly more than that in the first year."

The Ask:
After presenting the investment, stop talking. Let them process. Then:
Closer: "So, what are you thinking? Does this feel like the right move for you?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: This is a soft close, not a hard close. You are asking them to tell you where they are. If they say yes, move to payment. If they raise an objection, handle it using Section 3. If they say "I need to think about it," that is an objection - see the playbook. Do not let "I need to think about it" end the conversation.

DISCOUNT POLICY: No ad-hoc discounts. If a structured incentive exists (workshop/webinar attendee offer), present it during price presentation, not as a last-resort concession. If no structured incentive applies, the price is the price. Our analysis shows that prospects who close do so based on the value they perceive, not the discount they receive. Unprompted discounts cost revenue on deals that would close at full price.

## Phase 7: Onboarding or Follow-Up (3-5 minutes)
If they say YES:
Send the payment link immediately while on the call.
Stay on the call while they complete payment.
Confirm receipt of payment.
Explain next steps: "I'm going to connect you with our onboarding team. They'll get you access to the platform and help you schedule your first coaching call."
Express genuine excitement: "I'm really excited for you. This is going to be great."
End the call. Do not keep talking after payment is processed. The decision is made. Lingering invites doubt.

If they say NO or NEED TIME:
See Section 5: Follow-Up Protocol for structured next steps based on the reason.

# SECTION 3: OBJECTION HANDLING PLAYBOOK

Core Principle: Every objection is either a real concern that needs to be addressed, or a smokescreen for a deeper concern they are not saying out loud. Your job is to figure out which one it is. The framework for every objection is the same: Acknowledge -> Isolate -> Resolve -> Re-ask.

## Objection 1: "I can't afford it" / "It's too expensive"
Frequency: Most common objection across all calls. Appears in ~70% of sales conversations.

Step 1: Acknowledge
Closer: "I totally hear you, and I appreciate you being honest about that. Money is a real consideration, especially when you're building a business. Can I ask you a question about that?"

Step 2: Isolate
Closer: "Is it that you don't see the value in what we'd be doing together, or is it more that the timing of the payments feels tight right now? Because those are two very different things, and I want to make sure I'm addressing the right one."

Step 3: Resolve
If it's a VALUE concern:
Closer: "You told me earlier that there's [$ amount from gap creation] sitting on the table that you're not capturing right now. This investment is a fraction of that. The question isn't whether you can afford to do this - it's whether you can afford not to. Every month without a solution is another [$X] left behind."

If it's a CASH FLOW concern:
Closer: "I get it. The payment plan is designed for exactly this situation. [Present the payment plan if not already covered.] And in our experience, most members have covered the cost of the program within the first 2-3 months through additional revenue. You said you charge [$X per visit] - this is [Y] visits per month. Is that something you can generate?"

Step 4: Re-ask
Closer: "If the money piece makes sense when you look at it that way, is there anything else holding you back from getting started?"

## Objection 2: "I need to talk to my spouse/partner"
Frequency: Second most common. Often a smokescreen for financial concern or personal uncertainty.

Step 1: Acknowledge
Closer: "Absolutely, I respect that. Decisions like this should involve the people who matter most. Can I share something that might help with that conversation?"

Step 2: Isolate
Closer: "Help me understand - is your spouse the kind of person who needs to hear the details themselves, or are they more looking to you and trusting your judgment on this?"

Step 3: Resolve
If spouse trusts their judgment:
Closer: "In my experience, these conversations go one of two ways. You can go home with a problem - 'Hey, I found this thing, what do you think?' Or you can go home with a solution - 'Hey, I found exactly what we've been looking for, and I made a commitment.' Partners tend to respond better to certainty than to uncertainty. If YOU believe this is the right move, that confidence is what they need to see."

Closer: "And here's what I'd offer: if you go home tonight and have that conversation, and for whatever reason it's a dealbreaker, reach out to me and we'll take care of it. No hard feelings. But I'd rather you leave this call with a solution than with another thing to think about. Does that feel fair?"

If spouse needs to be directly involved:
Closer: "Would it be possible to get them on the phone right now for a few minutes? I'd love to answer any questions they might have and make sure they feel as good about this as you do."

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: If the spouse is unavailable and must be involved, schedule a follow-up call within 48 hours with BOTH decision-makers present. Do not leave it open-ended. "When would be a good time for the three of us to connect? I have availability [specific times]."

## Objection 3: "It's not the right time" / "Maybe later"
Frequency: Third most common. Usually masks either financial concern or low urgency.

Step 1: Acknowledge
Closer: "Totally understand. Timing matters. Can I ask - what would need to change for it to be the right time?"

Step 2: Isolate
Listen carefully to the answer. It will usually reveal the real objection:
"After the holidays" / "After this busy season" - This is a stall. Address with urgency.
"When I have more money saved up" - This is a financial objection. Go back to Objection 1.
"When I'm further along in my business" - This is a readiness concern. Address directly.
"I want to try on my own first" - This is a self-sufficiency belief. Address the cost of trial and error.

Step 3: Resolve
For the "stall" version:
Closer: "Here's the thing - there will never be a perfect time. There's always going to be a reason to wait. But the people who get the best results are the ones who start when it's a little uncomfortable, because that discomfort is exactly what drives action. If we get started now, by the time [their event/season/date], you'll already have momentum instead of starting from zero."

For "I want to try on my own first":
Closer: "I respect the self-reliance. But can I ask you honestly - how long have you been thinking about this? [They answer.] So it's been [X months/years] and you're still at the same point. That's not because you're not smart or hardworking. It's because nobody taught you how to do this. You wouldn't expect a patient to rehab a torn ACL by watching YouTube videos. Same thing applies here."

## Objection 4: "I've tried coaching before and it didn't work"

Step 1: Acknowledge and Explore
Closer: "That's actually really helpful to know. Tell me about that experience - what did you invest in, what did they promise, and what went wrong?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: Let them vent. This is cathartic and builds trust. Listen for what specifically failed - was it too rigid? Not personalized? No follow-through? Poor community? These become your differentiation points.

Step 2: Differentiate
Closer: "I appreciate you sharing that. That's actually a big reason people come to us - they've tried something generic and it didn't work. Here's what makes us different: [address their specific complaint]. We've worked with over 2,000 clinicians over the past 9 years. Our coaches aren't business bros - they're PTs who own practices and have been exactly where you are. And you're not getting a one-size-fits-all plan. Your coach creates an individualized blueprint based on YOUR business, YOUR goals, and YOUR situation."

## Objection 5: "I'm not looking to invest - I just wanted information"

This should be caught by the setter qualification framework. If it still makes it to the call:

Closer: "Totally fair, and I appreciate you being upfront about that. I don't want to waste your time either. Let me ask you this - the reason you got on this call is because something about your business isn't where you want it to be. What is that one thing? If we could solve that one problem, would it change things for you?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: If they engage with this question and describe a real problem with real pain, you have an opening to run a compressed discovery and gap creation. If they deflect or say "I'm really just browsing," respect it: "No problem at all. We have a podcast, YouTube channel, and a book that are great free resources. When you're at a point where you want hands-on help, we'd love to have this conversation." End the call within 5 minutes. Do not give free consulting.

THE FREE CONSULTING RULE: Never give away the solution before commitment. You can diagnose the problem. You can quantify the cost of the problem. You can describe what the solution looks like at a high level. But do NOT hand over specific tactics, templates, pricing strategies, marketing plans, or hiring advice to someone who has not committed. If you solve their problem on the call, they have no reason to buy.

# SECTION 4: GUARANTEE LANGUAGE
## Mastermind Guarantee
Present this after the price, during objection handling if needed. Do not lead with the guarantee - it should be a confidence booster, not a crutch.

Closer: "Here's something that should give you confidence. We guarantee that if you do the work and don't add at least the cost of the program in additional revenue during the year we work together, we'll refund you the difference. The program costs roughly $21,000 over 12 months. Our average member adds over $80,000 in additional revenue. So we're not even close to that threshold with most people. But the guarantee is there because we stand behind what we do."

Closer: "The only thing we ask is that you actually do the work. That means attending your accountability group meetings, completing the onboarding process, going through the curriculum, and attending at least one of our two live events. If you do all of that and still don't see the results, we'll make it right. Fair enough?"

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: Frame the conditions as positive actions that drive results, not as fine print designed to get out of the guarantee. The prospect should hear: "If you engage with this program and it doesn't work, we'll refund you" - not "If you miss one meeting, the guarantee is void."
## Rainmaker Guarantee
The Rainmaker does not have a dollar-amount guarantee. Instead, offer a commitment guarantee:

Closer: "Here's what I can tell you. In the past decade, out of over 2,000 clinicians we've worked with, we know of exactly 3 who no longer have businesses - and those were for personal life reasons, not because the model didn't work. We don't take that lightly. Your coach is personally invested in your success, and our whole team is behind you. If at any point you feel like you're not getting value, we want to hear about it so we can fix it. We're not a program that takes your money and disappears."

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: This reframes the guarantee around the track record and commitment rather than a financial promise. It gives the prospect confidence without creating backend risk. If a prospect pushes hard for a money-back guarantee on Rainmaker, you can say: "We don't offer a money-back guarantee on the Rainmaker because at this stage, the results are so dependent on the individual's effort and timeline. What we do guarantee is that you'll have a team of people who care deeply about helping you succeed."

# SECTION 5: FOLLOW-UP PROTOCOL

Not every prospect closes on the first call. The follow-up protocol ensures that warm prospects don't fall through the cracks and that the closer maintains a structured cadence rather than ad-hoc check-ins.
## Scenario A: Spouse/Partner Conversation Needed
On the call: Schedule a specific follow-up time within 48 hours. "When can the three of us connect? I have [specific times]."
If they resist a specific time: "I'll follow up with you tomorrow afternoon to see how the conversation went. What's the best way to reach you - text or call?"
Day 1 (next day): Text or call. "Hey [name], just checking in - how did the conversation go last night?"
Day 3: If no response, one more touchpoint. "Hey [name], wanted to make sure you got my message. Still excited about the possibility of working together. Let me know if you have any questions I can help with."
Day 7: Final message. "Hey [name], I know life gets busy. If the timing isn't right, no hard feelings at all. I'm here whenever you're ready. In the meantime, [relevant free resource link]."
## Scenario B: Timing/Not Ready Now (But Interested)
On the call: Assign homework. "Here's what I'd like you to do before we talk again: [specific, relevant task - track your evals for 30 days, calculate your capacity utilization, write down your top 3 referral sources, etc.]. When you've done that, we'll reconnect and I'll show you exactly how that data connects to what we do."
Schedule a specific follow-up date. "Let's put 30 days on the calendar - [specific date]. I'll send you a calendar invite."
Day 14: Mid-point check-in. "Hey [name], just checking in on that homework. How's it going? Any questions?"
Day 30: Follow-up call. Open with the homework. Use the data they collected to run an updated gap creation.

[COACH NOTE] COACH NOTE: Homework serves two purposes: it gives the prospect something concrete to do (maintaining engagement) and it provides you with data for a stronger second call. A prospect who completes the homework is serious. A prospect who doesn't complete it was never going to buy.
## Scenario C: Hard No (Not Interested / Not a Fit)
On the call: Be gracious. "Totally understand. I appreciate your time today. If anything changes down the road, we're here."
Add them to the nurture sequence (podcast, newsletter, free content). Do not follow up personally unless they reach back out.
Tag them in CRM with the reason for the no-close (financial, timing, not qualified, information-gathering, competitor, etc.) so the pattern can be tracked.
## Scenario D: Financial Constraint (Wants To But Can't)
On the call: Validate the constraint. Don't push someone into financial hardship. "I respect that, and I don't want you to put yourself in a tough spot. Let's figure out a timeline that works."
Set a specific reconnect date (60-90 days out). "Let's revisit this in [month]. I'll check in with you then."
In the interim, point them to free resources that build continued engagement and trust.
